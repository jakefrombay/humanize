One setup you’ll see a lot with AI agents is something people call the controller worker pattern. Pretty simple idea, honestly. One main agent takes the user’s request, figures out what the goal actually is, then splits the job into smaller pieces.

What happens next is where it gets interesting. Instead of doing everything itself, that main controller hands those pieces to other agents. Each worker handles one kind of task. One might pull in data. Another thinks through the problem. A different one writes a summary or runs a tool. Everyone sticks to their lane.

The controller keeps watch over the whole process. It gathers what the workers produce, checks how the pieces fit together, and then turns that pile of outputs into the final answer the user sees.

Why go through the trouble? Because swapping parts becomes easy. If a worker needs an upgrade or you want a better one, you can change that piece without rebuilding the whole system.

Which is why teams lean on this pattern when AI jobs get messy. When a task needs several different skills working together, letting a group of focused agents share the work just tends to make things run smoother.

Another pattern people like to use with agents is something called the perception, decision, action loop. Sounds fancy at first. Honestly, it’s pretty simple once you see it working.

At the start of the cycle, the agent just looks around and gathers information. That’s the perception step. It might read a user’s question, pull data from an API, or grab signals from a sensor depending on what kind of system it’s running in. What matters is that it’s taking in fresh input instead of guessing.

Then comes the thinking part. During the decision step, the agent weighs what it just saw and figures out what to do next. Sometimes it relies on a reasoning model. Other times it follows a set of rules or a policy someone wrote earlier. Either way, it’s basically asking itself a simple question: given what I know right now, what move makes the most sense?

Next up is action.

Once the agent settles on a choice, it actually does the thing. Maybe it writes a reply to the user. Maybe it calls a tool, saves something to memory, or triggers another system. The exact move depends on the task, but the idea stays the same.

And then the loop starts again.

Because the agent keeps repeating this observe, decide, act rhythm, it can deal with situations that keep changing. New input comes in. The agent notices it, rethinks the situation, and adjusts what it does next. Simple cycle, but surprisingly flexible.

What gets overlooked is how often one agent just isn’t enough. Some problems are too big, too messy, or have too many moving parts for a single system to handle on its own.

That’s where the multi-agent collaboration model comes in. Instead of one agent doing everything, several independent agents work side by side on the same problem. Each one takes a different role. One might focus on planning the steps. Another double-checks the work. A third actually carries out the task.

The weird thing is how they coordinate. They pass messages back and forth, share notes, and respond to each other as the work unfolds. Not perfectly, of course. Sometimes they loop, correct each other, try again. Through that back-and-forth, the group slowly edges closer to an answer.

Simple idea. Harder to build well.

What makes it interesting is the mix of perspectives. One agent might spot an error another missed. A different one might rethink the plan entirely. That kind of internal conversation pushes the system toward a better result than a single agent usually reaches on its own.

You’ll see this setup in research assistants, complex simulations, and other distributed AI tools where several thinking components cooperate. One system alone can do a lot.

A small team of them? Sometimes they figure things out faster than you’d expect.
